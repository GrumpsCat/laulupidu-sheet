Sleep Sheet

This study examined whether parental reports of sleep problems in children with autism spectrum disorder (ASD) correspond to objective physiological sleep differences and whether these sleep patterns relate to daytime behavior. Fifty-eight children (ages 4–10) completed two nights of simultaneous actigraphy and polysomnography (PSG) along with standardized sleep and behavioral assessments. ASD “poor sleepers,” as rated by parents, showed significantly longer sleep latency and greater sleep fragmentation, along with reduced sleep efficiency and increased wake after sleep onset on actigraphy. These children also exhibited higher levels of inattention, hyperactivity, and repetitive/compulsive behaviors, with fragmentation correlating strongly with ritualistic and restrictive behaviors. Results demonstrate that parental sleep concerns reliably map onto quantifiable sleep phenotypes, positioning PSG and actigraphy as complementary methods for defining sleep dysfunction and guiding targeted interventions.

Reference: Goldman, S. E., Surdyka, K., Cuevas, R., Adkins, K., Wang, L., & Malow, B. A. (2009). Defining the sleep phenotype in children with autism. Developmental Neuropsychology, 34(5), 560–573.

This article proposes that Parkinson’s disease (PD) results not only from dopamine loss and α-synuclein pathology, but from a fundamental failure to maintain separation between sleep and wake states. PD is reframed as a pathological “superposition” in which REM-like features—such as muscle atonia, dream-like cognition, and autonomic instability—intrude into wakefulness, while wake-like activity disrupts normal sleep. Sustained cortical overactivation destabilizes sleep–wake circuitry, forming a vicious cycle of mixed states. A secondary loop involving impaired glymphatic clearance and neuroinflammation accelerates degeneration. This model explains PD’s motor, cognitive, and autonomic symptoms as consequences of chronic hybrid states and suggests that restoring sleep architecture may slow progression.

Reference: Greenup, G. (2025). Parkinson’s disease as a superposition of wake and sleep states: a hypothesis of vicious cycle neurodegeneration. Medical Hypotheses, 205, 111796. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mehy.2025.111796

Polysomnography (PSG) – A comprehensive overnight sleep study measuring EEG, breathing, movement, oxygen saturation, and sleep stages.

Actigraphy – A wrist-worn motion sensor that estimates sleep and wake states based on movement patterns across the night.

Sleep Latency – The amount of time it takes to fall asleep after lights out; prolonged latency indicates difficulty initiating sleep.

Sleep Efficiency – The percentage of time in bed actually spent asleep, used as a broad measure of sleep quality.

Wake After Sleep Onset (WASO) – The total duration of awakenings occurring after initial sleep onset, reflecting sleep continuity.

Glymphatic Clearance – A sleep-dependent system that removes metabolic waste from the brain through cerebrospinal fluid pathways and glial channels.

Nigra and α-Synuclein – Refers to the substantia nigra, which produces dopamine, and α-synuclein, a neuronal protein whose aggregation is associated with neurodegeneration.

Brainstem Autonomy – The brainstem’s ability to regulate essential functions such as breathing, arousal, reflexes, and sleep–wake transitions independently.

REM Atonia – Temporary muscle paralysis during REM sleep that prevents acting out dreams.

Arousal Systems – Brainstem networks (e.g., reticular activating system) that maintain wakefulness and regulate attention.

Fragmentation Index – An actigraphy-derived measurement of nighttime restlessness capturing movement-related microarousals.

Repetitive Behavior Scale–Revised (RBS-R) – A standardized measure of stereotyped, compulsive, ritualistic, and restrictive behaviors in ASD.

Child Behavior Checklist (CBCL) – A parent-report tool assessing daytime emotional and behavioral problems such as inattention and hyperactivity.

Children’s Sleep Habits Questionnaire (CSHQ) – A validated survey assessing insomnia, parasomnias, sleep anxiety, and night wakings.

Sleep Phenotype – A measurable profile of sleep characteristics that defines a subgroup (e.g., ASD poor sleepers vs. good sleepers).

Parental Concerns Questionnaire (PCQ) – A questionnaire used to classify children as “good” or “poor” sleepers based on parental concern severity.

Cholinergic Modulation – Acetylcholine-driven signaling important for attention, learning, REM sleep generation, and arousal.

Dopaminergic Signaling – Neural communication using dopamine, essential for movement control, reward processing, and motivation.

Autonomic Regulation – Control of unconscious physiological functions such as heart rate, digestion, and blood pressure.

Sleep Architecture – The normal pattern of sleep stages, including REM and NREM cycles, across a typical night.

REM–NREM Cycling – The alternating pattern of REM sleep and non-REM sleep that organizes normal sleep structure.

Locus Coeruleus Activity – Noradrenergic signaling originating from the locus coeruleus, influencing arousal, stress responses, attention, and sleep–wake regulation.
