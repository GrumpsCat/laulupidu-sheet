Summary of the Hypothesis – This article proposes that Parkinson’s disease (PD) results not only from dopamine loss and α-synuclein pathology, but from a fundamental failure to maintain separation between sleep and wake states. The author suggests that PD arises from a pathological “superposition” in which REM-like features—such as muscle atonia, dream-like cognition, and autonomic instability—intrude into wakefulness, while wake-like activity disrupts normal sleep. Sustained cortical overactivation destabilizes sleep–wake circuitry, forming a vicious cycle of mixed states. A secondary loop involving impaired glymphatic clearance and neuroinflammation accelerates degeneration. This model explains PD’s motor, cognitive, and autonomic symptoms as consequences of chronic hybrid states and suggests that restoring sleep architecture may slow progression. (Greenup, 2025)

Glymphatic Clearance – A sleep-dependent system that removes metabolic waste from the brain through cerebrospinal fluid pathways and glial channels.

Nigra and α-Synuclein – Refers to the substantia nigra, which produces dopamine, and α-synuclein, a neuronal protein whose aggregation is associated with neurodegeneration.

Brainstem Autonomy – The brainstem’s ability to regulate essential functions such as breathing, arousal, reflexes, and sleep–wake transitions independently.

REM Atonia – Temporary muscle paralysis during REM sleep that prevents acting out dreams.

Arousal Systems – Brainstem networks (e.g., reticular activating system) that maintain wakefulness and regulate attention.

Cholinergic Modulation – Acetylcholine-driven signaling important for attention, learning, REM sleep generation, and arousal.

Dopaminergic Signaling – Neural communication using dopamine, essential for movement control, reward processing, and motivation.

Autonomic Regulation – Control of unconscious physiological functions such as heart rate, digestion, and blood pressure.

Sleep Architecture – The normal pattern of sleep stages, including REM and NREM cycles, across a typical night.

REM–NREM Cycling – The alternating pattern of REM sleep and non-REM sleep that organizes normal sleep structure.
